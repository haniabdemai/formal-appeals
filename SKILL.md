# Formal appeals and institutional disputes

Every rule here was earned through rejected drafts in real appeals. A new session with no context can follow this file alone. Follow the order. (All quoted letter lines and figures in this file are illustrative examples; the lessons are what shipped.)

## Before drafting a single sentence

1. **Read the original sources, not summaries.** The decision letter (scan/photo), every email in the actual mailbox, every document previously submitted. Project reference files drift from reality: a summary may state one deadline when the original letter says another.
2. **Email search mixes unsent drafts into threads.** Before citing any message as sent, verify it was actually sent (in Gmail, check `labelIds` contains `SENT`). AI-generated draft replies can sit in the mailbox and look real. An argument was once built on a never-sent email; the user caught it, not the checks.
3. **Verify every number against its source document.** Recompute totals; check whether figures are gross or net, annual or monthly, projected or actual — mismatched bases invite contradiction. If the institution misquotes the user's own figure, correct it quietly and attribute: "the figure of £X quoted in your letter is evidently the amount stated in my [source document], which is in fact £Y".
4. **Find the institution's own rules and requests, and hold them to those.** The institution's published staff guidance, internal guidance released under freedom-of-information requests, their own request emails. The strongest ground is always: "your own email of [date] asked for X; I supplied X by your deadline."
5. **Ask the user what the point is before building arguments.** The user knows why the decision is wrong; do not invent the theory of the case. Never decide what personal documents to disclose (medical, HMRC records): that is the user's call alone.

## Structure: conclusion first, then the road to it

The principle: tell the reader the main point, then explain how you arrive at that conclusion, then restate what you want at the end.

- **Para 1:** what this is, in the user's own word for it (appeal, complaint, reconsideration — use what they say), the route and window quoted from the decision letter, plus the institution's process phrase once ("I ask for it to be treated as a formal request for reconsideration").
- **Para 2:** the grounds, numbered, summarised in a sentence each, ending "I set out each ground below, followed by what I am asking you to do." Promise N grounds and deliver exactly N; a count mismatch is the kind of error that survives to late review.
- **Background before Timeline.** The reader must know who the person is and what the situation costs before the dates mean anything. Identity first, in the institution's own nomenclature — use whatever categories they use to describe the applicant. Then why they applied: capability first, difficulty second: lead with what the person can do, then the specific barrier.
- **Timeline as a dated list.** Bold date, one entry per line, every entry naming its medium ("in an email"), verbatim quotes for the decisive lines. Answer the reader's obvious questions inside it (why silences happened, what arrived when).
- **A resolution device if the story allows one.** Look for a way to turn an apparent weakness in the user's timeline into the letter's reason for existing — e.g. if there was a delayed reply, the appeal itself can become that reply.
- **Answer every question they asked, even inapplicable ones**, in a dedicated section. It removes the reviewer's last excuse.
- **The ask, restating the conclusion:** accept the claim; fallback = a single list of anything missing, why it is needed, where to send it, and a clear deadline; written confirmation of receipt; preferred contact method stated.

## Subheading titles: use legal-document register, not narrative register

Rejected as childish (narrative register is not taken seriously by reviewers):
- "What happened, in order" → **Timeline**
- "Why I am asking you to look again" → **Grounds of appeal**
- "The questions, answered" (no context for a cold reader) → **Response to the information requested on [date] and [date]**

The full set that survived: **Background / Timeline / Grounds of appeal / Response to the information requested on [dates] / What I am asking for**. Titles carry dates and specifics; a reader with zero context must understand each from the title alone.

## Wording: before and after (each "before" was rejected)

| Rejected | Shipped | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Here is what happened." / "Some background first, because it matters" | (deleted; subheadings do this) | Meta-scaffolding announces instead of doing; reads AI-generated |
| "That makes no sense to me" | "I believe the information the criteria call for had been received, and confirmed as received, before the decision was made" | State the case, not the feeling; too combative |
| "everything they actually need" | "the information required under the eligibility criteria, set out in your email of [date]" | "Actually" reads sassy; every claim needs an attributed source |
| "I will be candid: I did not register that deadline" | "I did not register that deadline when it arrived." | Just say the thing; no performative honesty markers |
| "a CV is a sales document" | "the CV reflects the position as it stands" | Quips are what a hostile reader remembers |
| "I also want to be fair: my contact there was courteous throughout" | (deleted) | Not-argumentative does not mean apologetic; neutral is the register |
| "with a deadline in its final line, a few days later" | "a deadline added at the very bottom of the message… This effectively left only [N] working days." | Concreteness ("very bottom", "effectively left only") does the arguing |
| "what needs to be established for an applicant like me" | "for someone in [specific circumstance]" | Say what you mean; "like me" is vague |
| "I provided more than enough information" | "I have provided everything your criteria require, and more" | Same content, without the petulance |
| "please tell me exactly what it is… tell me… and set a deadline" | "please set it out in a single list, explaining what is needed and why, where I should send it, and a clear deadline" | An imperative stack hectors; one calm request |

## Connecting words and flow

Staccato declaratives read machine-written. Use connectors: **However, Additionally, It is also worth noting, For completeness, Which brings me to**. Example pattern:

Before (choppy): "They asked for X. They already had it. They said nothing else was needed. Then they asked for more."
After (connected): "They asked for X when it had already been supplied, and a written assurance that nothing further was needed was followed a week later by additional requests."

Also: vary sentence openings; do not repeat a pointed word ("your own…" four times reads combative; once is pointed); do not use the same verb five times ("evidence" as a verb); one candid personal line stated once, plainly, beats a paragraph of pathos.

## Tone summary

Formal-but-human. NOT chatty email voice transplanted into a letter; NOT barrister cadence; NOT apologetic. Hard bans for all output, including generated documents like pack indexes: no em-dashes, no "X, not Y" antithesis, no punchy fragments, no aphorisms, no sassy "actually". Where relevant legislation applies, cite it in two or three sentences alongside the institution's own published guidance. Constructive, never threatening. Do not hand the institution an alternative explanation that undermines the user's core case (e.g. mentioning unrelated commitments when the real cause was a circumstance the institution should have accommodated).

## Deliverables set

Letter (docx + pdf, versioned filename), cover sheet on the institution's own template if one exists, phone script (what to say, note-taking table, fallbacks), print-and-post checklist, sources file (every claim mapped to a link or quote), supporting document pack.

## Supporting document pack

One PDF: contents page, then a title page before every document, then the documents. Rules learned:
- Contents page like a typed contents page: dot-leader tabs to page numbers, no em-dashes, no "·" separators (both read AI-generated to the user).
- Page numbers stamped through the WHOLE bundle ("Pack page N of [total]", bottom right, small and grey, distinct from documents' internal numbering) so the contents page is usable on paper. reportlab overlay + pypdf merge.
- Emails one per page in email-client print style: bold subject, bold sender, grey date and to lines, clean paragraphs. Strip quoted thread history (cut at two-line "On … wrote:" AND at the first ">" line) and boilerplate footers, with an honesty note that content is otherwise unaltered.
- Photos of letters fitted to A4 with borders via img2pdf, never raw image dimensions.
- Build every generated part with docx-js then convert to PDF. LibreOffice's HTML import mangles tables, floats and page breaks; never use HTML for anything with layout.
- Number the pack sections to match the letter's enclosure numbers exactly, and say so on the contents page.

## Iteration discipline

- **Never regenerate a delivered file in place.** New versioned filename every revision. The user comments inside the docx: before touching anything, unpack it and read `word/comments.xml` plus the anchored text in `document.xml`.
- **Before declaring done:** adversarial review by two independent subagents (a subject-matter/legal-adviser lens and a prose/AI-tell lens), each given the rendered text plus a verified fact sheet. Fix every HIGH finding.
- **Visually verify every rendered page after every regeneration.** Validation passing is not layout working.
- User feedback rounds are specific and final. Apply exactly; do not re-litigate; do not overcorrect into the opposite failure (told "less argumentative", a draft may swing to apologetic — that is equally wrong).
